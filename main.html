The Workshop

Biological muscles are among the most sophisticated actuators in nature. They are not single force generators but excitable, adaptive structures that integrate mechanical compliance, distributed sensing, and rhythm generation within a single material system. In this sense, muscles "think" through their nonlinear dynamics and material structure, achieving embodied intelligent behavior without centralized computation. Despite progress in artificial muscle and soft robotics, engineered systems have yet to fully utilize these biological principles. Actuation and intelligence are still commonly designed as separate modules, and the nonlinear dynamics that give biological muscle its versatility remain underexplored as a design principle for robotic systems.

This workshop brings together researchers from biological muscular models, stimuli-responsive materials, and soft robotic systems to examine a unifying question:

How can the autonomous dynamics of biological muscles and responsive materials inform the next generation of embodied intelligence in soft robotics?
